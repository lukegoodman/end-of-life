First days

Let people know

When someone dies it's important to let the right people know as quickly as you can. The authorities you'll need to contact will depend on how the person died, as each situation will be handled differently.

You may also need to tell family, whānau, friends and the person's employer if they were working.

When someone dies in a hospital, hospice or rest home you'll need to:

  • alert medical or rest home staff on duty.

The medical or rest home staff will contact the doctor who'll check to confirm the cause of death. If the cause of death is known and from natural causes they'll be able to complete a Medical Certificate of Cause of Death. You must have this certificate before you or a funeral director can go ahead with a burial or cremation.

Usually you'll be able to stay with the body until you feel comfortable to call someone - for example a funeral director of your choice.

  When someone dies in care

If the cause of death is not obvious, or suspected to be from unnatural causes, further investigation will be required. In this case the doctor will need to report the death to a coroner.

  What happens when a death is referred to a coroner

You'll probably need to collect the person's personal belongings. If there are certain items you want to stay with your loved one, such as a wedding ring, you'll need to make sure you let the staff know.

Choose a funeral director

Choosing a funeral director is an important decision to make after someone dies. You don't have to use a funeral director if you don't want - you can organise everything yourself.

If you decide to use a funeral director this should be one of the first things you organise.

A funeral director will help you to organise a funeral service and deal with legal requirements.

They can take care of everything, or you can choose to do some things yourself.

Most funeral directors' services include:

  • working with authorities and registering the death
  • transporting the person's body
  • meeting the family and whānau to understand their wishes
  • embalming, care and presentation of the person's body
  • organising the burial or cremation
  • recommending or arranging a celebrant
  • arranging the funeral service and any function afterwards.

In New South Wales anyone can act as a funeral director. It's a good idea to check what requirements and qualifications a funeral home has before you choose one.

You can ask if a funeral home belongs to the Australian Funeral Directors Association (AFDA) or New South Wales Funeral Directors Association. Many funeral homes are part of these associations as they follow certain standards and codes.

Asking family and friends who they have used in the past is a good way to find a funeral director. You can also look in the yellow pages or online on the NSWFDA or AFDA websites.

Funeral directors may charge a flat fee for their services, or they may charge for each service individually. Some services may include additional costs passed on from a provider - such as florists or newspapers.

You'll need to check what you're paying for first by asking for an itemised quote that includes any additional costs. You can get a quote in person, over the phone or online.

Find funeral packages

Funeral Directors Association of Australia have an online form where you can request funeral cost estimates from funeral homes in your area. They'll organise estimates from as many funeral homes as you choose. The funeral homes you choose will get back to you directly.

Costs involved

You may feel overwhelmed thinking of all the costs involved when someone close to you dies. It's important to remember that you don't have to agree to anything that's outside of your budget.

Costs you must pay:

  • fees for medical specialist to confirm the death
  • a casket or shroud (sheet)
  • fees for burial or cremation.

Extra costs you might pay:

  • having a service
  • a headstone or memorial
  • fees for looking after the person's body (such as embalming and transport)
  • fee paid to a funeral director
  • fee for legal help to deal with the person's estate.

It's important to know that:

  • expenses are usually paid from the estate (money from everything owned) of the person who died
  • the person might've had insurance or a funeral trust with money in it
  • funeral costs are usually the first expense paid from the estate
  • if funeral costs are paid by the family or whānau before the person's money is released from their estate, they'll usually get refunded.

    • If the person who died didn't own much or have a lot of money, they might not have enough money in their estate to pay for funeral costs. If there isn't enough money in the estate, family and whānau will have to meet any costs.

    You should keep these things in mind before planning a service to make sure there'll be enough money to pay for it.

You can:

  • get estimates from different funeral homes
  • check the casket types and prices as they can vary
  • consider cremation, which usually costs less than burial as there are lower fees
  • check if you're eligible to get a government grant
  • check if you're eligible to get cheaper or free legal advice.

Take bereavement leave

Bereavement leave is paid leave you can take from work when someone close to you dies and you meet certain criteria. You'll need notify your employer as soon as possible after the bereavement to let them know:

  • what's happened
  • that you won't be able to come into work
  • that you want to apply for bereavement leave.

You'll only get paid for bereavement leave if you would've normally worked on the day leave is taken.

You can use bereavement leave for any type of employment (permanent, fixed-term, part-time and casual) if:

  • you've worked for your employer continuously for six months or:
  • you've worked for your employer for six months for:
  • an average of 10 hours per week, and
  • at least one hour in every week or 40 hours in every month.

If you've been at your job for at least 6 months and meet the criteria, you can take a minimum of:

  • 3 days' paid leave after the death of a close family or whānau member, or
  • 1 days' paid leave after the death of another person if your employer accepts you've had a bereavement.

Some employers offer more than 3 days’ leave — you should check your employment agreement. You can take this leave at any time and for any reason that relates to the death.

If you haven't been at your job for at least 6 months, or don't meet the criteria, your employer may still agree to give you leave. You'll need to discuss your options with them.

Find a will

If the person who died left a will it's important to find it early. It may express their funeral preferences or how they want their body cared for. If you talked to them about their will you may already know where to find it.

What to expect from the will

If you don't know where to look you could try:

  • asking their lawyer
  • looking around their house
  • asking a trustee organisation – for example the Public Trust
  • asking a lawyer to check the NSW Will Registry

Where to find a will

If you find the will is being held by a lawyer or trustee organisation, they'll probably only let the executor view it. Other people will need to get permission from the executor to view the will.

If the person didn't leave a will, they may have talked about their funeral preferences or written them down. If they haven't left anything, you must make these decisions with your family.

Choose a final resting place

These decisions could be guided by culture, religion or family tradition.

If the person who died made their wishes clear about their final resting place, then some of these decisions will already be made for you and your family.

Anyone - not just a funeral director - can organise the burial or cremation of a person's body.

Before burial or cremation

Burial provides a special place where family and friends can visit and remember someone who has died.

Costs involved in burial

  • buying a plot
  • an interment fee - which covers the cost of digging the grave and on-going maintenance
  • creation of a memorial or headstone.

A body can be buried

  • in a public burial ground like a cemetery
  • in a private burial ground like a church
  • in a natural/eco-friendly burial ground
  • in a religious/denominational burial ground
  • at sea.

Cemeteries are managed by local councils and each will have their own rules and regulations - which you'll need to check. If you're using a funeral director they can help you organise this.

Cremation is a heat process which reduces a body to ashes. Cremation usually happens after a funeral service and can take between 1-3 hours. The ashes are returned in a simple plastic container. You can then decide what you want to do with them.

Costs involved in cremation

  • a fee for the cremation
  • an interment fee for burying the ashes - if you choose this option
  • a fee for placing the ashes in a niche at a cemetery - if you choose this option
  • creation of a memorial or headstone.

Ashes can be

  • scattered
  • buried
  • kept in an urn
  • shared among family – each person has a smaller amount of ashes
  • placed in a niche at a cemetery.

Before a body can be buried or cremated in New South Wales, the person taking care of these arrangements must get either a doctor’s Medical Certificate of Cause of Death or a coroner’s authorisation - there may be a cost for this.

Before a burial you'll need to complete an application to bury.

Before a cremation you'll need to complete an application to cremate and other forms to make sure there's no reason the cremation shouldn't go ahead.

If you're using a funeral director they'll do everything for you.

If you're doing this yourself you'll have to complete the application forms. You can get the correct forms from your local council which runs the cemetery or crematorium – either online or from their office.

Funeral or memorial service

When the casket is at the service, it's a funeral service. A funeral service doesn't have to happen immediately, as embalming or chilling can preserve the body for the funeral.

When the casket isn't at the service, it's a memorial service. A memorial service can happen anywhere and can be weeks or months after a death.

There's no legal requirement to have either a funeral or memorial service.

Plan a service

Funeral or memorial services are a time to gather together to celebrate the life of someone who's died. They give the opportunity for family, whānau and friends to say goodbye and provide social support for the grieving process.

A service can be as complex or as simple as you want. There are many different options that you and your family or whānau can choose to include in a service.

Funeral service options

Funeral services directory

If you want to organise the service yourself you could:

  • talk to family and friends about their own experiences
  • look around your community
  • find suitable options online.

If you're using a funeral director they can guide you through these choices.

Choose a funeral director

A celebrant is any person who helps to plan, structure and lead a ceremony. If you'd like to have a funeral or memorial service, or other ceremony, you may wish to have a celebrant.

This could be:

  • a professional celebrant
  • a minister
  • a family member
  • a friend

If you want to have a family or a friend help you plan, structure and lead the ceremony, you'll have to talk to them about it.

If you want to have a professional celebrant help you plan, structure and lead the ceremony, you can:

  • ask family and friends who they've used in the past
  • talk to your funeral director if you're using one

There will be a fee for using a professional celebrant.
